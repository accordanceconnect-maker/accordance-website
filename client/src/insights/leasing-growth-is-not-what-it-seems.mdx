## The Misread

Retail leasing across NCR has accelerated.

At a glance, this looks like expansion. More deals. More activity. More movement.

The conclusion being drawn is straightforward: demand is strong.

But that is not what the current cycle is showing.

What appears as growth is, in many cases, execution catching up with availability.

And that distinction changes how these transactions should be read.

---

## What Is Driving the Increase

For an extended period, quality retail supply remained limited.

Demand did not disappear during this phase. It remained active, but constrained.

Brands evaluated locations. Negotiations progressed. Decisions were taken.

But closures were delayed.

As new Grade A and Grade A+ assets became operational, that constraint eased.

Transactions that were pending began to move.

Leasing activity increased.

Not because demand expanded suddenly, but because it was finally able to transact.

---

## Leasing Velocity vs Demand Strength

Leasing volume measures transaction activity. It does not measure demand quality.

When supply improves:

- Options increase  
- Deal cycles shorten  
- Closures accelerate  

This creates a visible rise in leasing velocity.

However, this does not necessarily indicate:

- Higher store productivity  
- Stronger consumer demand  
- Sustainable rental growth  

Leasing numbers tend to move faster than underlying fundamentals.

Confusing the two leads to mispriced decisions.

---

## Where the Market Is Actually Moving

The current cycle is not uniform.

Transactions are concentrating in:

- Established high streets  
- Dominant, well-positioned malls  
- Corridors with proven catchments  

At the same time:

- Secondary assets are competing for tenants  
- Incentives are increasing in select locations  
- Tenant selection has become more disciplined  

The market is not expanding evenly.

It is adjusting to where supply is viable and outcomes are predictable.

---

## The Risk of Following Volume

In periods of rising activity, speed becomes a default response.

Expansion teams move faster. Developers read velocity as validation. Capital begins to price in continuity.

This is where misalignment begins.

Entering based on momentum often leads to:

- Elevated entry pricing  
- Overestimation of catchment depth  
- Increased competitive density  
- Longer stabilization periods  

The transaction completes. The performance takes longer to align.

---

## What Has Structurally Changed

The underlying structure of the retail market has shifted.

Supply has improved. Choice has returned. Selectivity has increased.

Premium assets continue to absorb with clarity. Mid-tier assets are competing for position. High streets are holding pricing, but with emerging pressure.

Leasing activity will continue to remain visible.

But what that activity represents has changed.

---

## What This Means for Transactions

For retail expansion, leasing momentum is no longer a sufficient signal.

Decisions now require:

- Corridor-level evaluation before commitment  
- Supply pipeline visibility  
- Entry timing aligned with asset positioning  
- Lease structures that reflect downside risk  

For developers, leasing velocity is no longer a proxy for asset strength.

Stabilization will vary significantly across asset tiers.

For capital, transaction volume is not an indicator of income stability.

Asset-level performance and supply dynamics now drive outcomes.

---

## Closing Perspective

Leasing growth is visible.

What is less visible is what is driving it.

This is not a uniform demand expansion. It is a transaction cycle adjusting to new supply.

In this phase, activity increases. But outcomes diverge.

The difference is no longer in who participates.

It is in how precisely they position within the cycle.
